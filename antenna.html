Antenna

- Capture satellites images -

As part of my major, we built a low-tech device to intercept signals from old satellites. The project focused on receiving transmissions from NOAA weather satellites, which operate in the 137 - 138 MHz range and use Right Hand Circular Polarization (RHCP). Our goal was to construct a simple yet effective receiving system using basic materials and accessible technology.

For the antenna, we chose a V-dipole design, which offered a balance between ease of construction and reliable reception. Each dipole element was cut to approximately 53.4 cm, optimized for the target frequency. We mounted the antenna in a horizontal plane to minimize interference from vertically polarized signals, such as commercial radio transmissions.

Once the antenna was assembled, we connected it to an RTL-SDR (Software-Defined Radio) dongle via a coaxial cable. The SDR allowed us to tune into different frequencies and process the received signals using a computer. To decode the satellite transmissions, we used WXtoImg, a specialized program for processing NOAA satellite images.

The first test was a complete failure. Our satellite image was filled with too much noise and only displayed a small portion of the expected transmission. To obtain a clear image, we had to capture the satellite's signals three separate times, which significantly extended the process.
This delay was mainly due to the need for precise timing and orientation. We had to carefully check the satellite pass schedules to determine when they would be directly above us. Additionally, we needed to constantly adjust the antenna's direction to ensure optimal signal reception, making the process both time-consuming and challenging.

On our last trial, we were finally able to capture a good image with minimal noise. While some interference remained, the overall result was remarkably clear. This time, the satellite transmission revealed a wide view of Western Europe, including France, Italy, the UK, and surrounding regions. The level of detail in the image was impressive, allowing us to distinguish cloud formations, coastlines, and major landmasses.

Lastly, we took the satellite images, inverted the colors to create a negative, and printed them onto transparent film. Using these negatives, we experimented with **cyanotype printing**, a photographic process that uses sunlight to develop images.
By placing the transparencies on light-sensitive paper and exposing them to the sun, we created deep blue-toned prints of the satellite images. This final step transformed our digital captures into physical artworks, merging technology with traditional photographic techniques to produce unique visual representations of the satellite data.

Overall, this project demonstrated that with a well-positioned V-dipole antenna, a simple SDR receiver, and proper signal processing, it is possible to intercept and decode satellite transmissions using a low-tech approach. This hands-on experience reinforced our understanding of radio signal propagation, antenna design, and signal decoding, making it a valuable part of our studies.
