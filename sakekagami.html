Sakekagami

- Connection and reconnection to tradition, to others, and to ourselves. -

My project began with a simple question: "How can technology bring us closer to tradition, to others, and to ourselves?"

In continuity with my vision to bridge technology and tradition I started asking my self which direction I wanted to explore next. As I was in Kyoto, the spiritual heart of Japan, surrounded by Shinto Shrines, I got quite interested into shinto beliefs and rituals.
As I learned more about Japanese rituals and traditions, one word kept returning to me â€” connection.

One of the centeral element that caught my attention was Sake, a traditional Japanese rice wine, often used in rituals.
Sake is more than just a drink; it is a symbol of connection, a medium through which people come together to share moments, stories, and memories.
In ancient times, the process of fermentation invisible to the human eye was believed to be the work of Gods, transforming rice into a sacred beverage.
Sake was used in ceremonies to connect with the divine, purify the body and spirits, and build bonds.

One other element i was inspired by was the Mirror. An element that have a strong significance in Shinto religion as a symbol of truth and self-reflection.
The mirror is believed to reflect not just physical appearance but also the inner self, encouraging individuals to look inward and connect with their true nature.
One specific type of mirror that intrigued me was the "Mizukagami", which translates to water (mizu) mirror (kagami).
This natural mirror forms on the surface of still water, reflecting the surrounding environment and creating a serene and contemplative space. The water does not just mirror the present, but evokes the passing of time, the change of seasons, and a connection between heaven and earth. Some believed that water mirrors acted as portals to the other world, linking us with those who lived before us.

As you might have guessed from the name, this project "Sakekagami" is a fusion of these two concepts: Sake and Mizukagami.
I thought what if I could create a mirror portal throught a cup of sake that connects us to tradition, to others, and to ourselves.
A mirror that connects the past and the present, the physical and the spiritual, the individual and the collective.
A cup that forges bonds across time and space, letting us share a drink and connect.

Each custom masu cup is equipped with a Raspberry Pi, a miniature camera, and a small display. The cups communicate over a network: the camera in each cup captures the user's face, which is processed via TouchDesigner and StreamDiffusion to generate real-time images. These visuals are then projected inside the opposite cup, letting users encounter one another through a digital reflection.
