NeuroAesthetic

- Making Art With Your Brain -

Inspired by my Japanese cultural heritage and childhood memories, I wanted to create an interactive art installation, that bridges tradition and modernity.

I thought, “What if the shoji paper windows could serve as a canvas to display mental states?”

By combining incense as an olfactory guide and EEG technology, my goal was to visualize brainwave patterns on shoji paper windows to create a dynamic and meditative ambiance. The installation fosters mindfulness and introspection, blending tradition, technology, and innovative art.

I used the Muse 2 headset, a commercial EEG device designed to track electrical signals generated by brain activity.
This headset captures brainwave data, enabling the real-time monitoring and analysis of mental states such as relaxation, focus, and mindfulness, which serve as the foundation for creating interactive and immersive visualizations in the installation.

By connecting the Muse 2 headset to a computer, we were able to collect raw, unfiltered electrical signals generated by brain activity.
Using Python, we processed these signals and filtered them into five distinct brainwave types based on their frequency ranges:
- Delta (0.5 - 4 Hz), associated with deep relaxation and sleep;
- Theta (4 - 8 Hz), linked to creativity and meditation;
- Alpha (8 - 12 Hz), representing calmness and readiness;
- Beta (12 - 30 Hz), connected to active thinking and focus;
- Gamma (30 - 100 Hz), associated with cognitive functions and heightened awareness.

We sent the filtered data via Open Sound Control (OSC) on port 5000, which was then received by TouchDesigner—a node-based visual programming software used for real-time graphics, interactive media, and data visualization.
Once the data was successfully transmitted, we had to first become familiar with TouchDesigner's interface and workflow.
This allowed us to create visuals that respond dynamically to the EEG sensor data, enabling real-time interaction and artistic expression.

In our project, we developed a series of prototypes exploring the visualization of brainwave activity through immersive and interactive media.
Here is one example of visuals created using TouchDesigner, where we generated three point clouds—red, blue, and green—each controlled by different brainwave frequencies: alpha, gamma, and delta, respectively.
The density of points in each cloud dynamically changes based on the frequency of the corresponding brainwaves, creating an evolving, meditative visual representation of cognitive activity. This prototype serves as an early step in translating brainwave data into an artistic and sensory experience.

To explore how the visuals would appear on shoji windows, I created a small-scale reproduction of a door using wood. This prototype serves as a tangible testbed for projecting brainwave-driven visuals onto a traditional paper surface, allowing me to observe how light and patterns interact with the material.

The next step will be to create a full-sized door and develop additional visuals to enhance the experience.
I would also like to explore the possibility of using a 360-degree screen for a fully immersive environment, allowing the visuals to extend beyond the shoji and envelop the viewer in a meditative, brainwave-driven space.
